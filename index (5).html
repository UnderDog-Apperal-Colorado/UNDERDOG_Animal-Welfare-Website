// The mission

One directory. Every kind of org. No pitting shelters against each other.

UNDERDOG CO exists to make it easier for Colorado donors, volunteers, and adopters to find and support animal welfare organizations — and to make sure the orgs carrying the heaviest, most thankless load aren't invisible next to the ones with the biggest social media budgets.

A necessary disclaimer

This site is not here to shame or steer people away from open-admission (also called "open intake") shelters. Open-admission shelters take in every animal a community sends them — strays, bite cases, litters, seized cruelty animals, owner surrenders with nowhere else to go — with no ability to say no. That job is essential, often underfunded, and it's the safety net everything else in this ecosystem depends on. Nothing on this site should be read as "avoid the open-admission shelter." Support them too.

// Know the terms before you judge a shelter

What "no-kill" actually means

These terms get thrown around online with a lot of moral heat and not a lot of definition. Here's the honest, unglamorous version of both models, based on Colorado's own regulatory language and how the sheltering field defines these terms nationally.

Open-admission

Takes every animal, every time

  • Legally or contractually obligated to accept any animal brought in — often municipal shelters under city/county contract, or the designated cruelty-holding facility for their area.
  • Cannot turn away a bite case, an aggressive animal, a sick or injured stray, or a surrender because "we're full."
  • Because they can't control intake, their live-release rate is directly shaped by how much community support (fosters, rescue partners, adopters, donations) they get — not by how much they care.
  • Example locally: Denver Animal Shelter is the City and County of Denver's open-admission municipal shelter.
Limited-admission / "no-kill"

Chooses intake by capacity

  • Usually a private nonprofit — not contractually bound to take every animal. Intake is by appointment, waitlist, transfer, or owner-surrender screening.
  • Can pause intake when full, which is exactly what protects a high live-release rate (the industry benchmark for "no-kill" is generally a 90%+ live-release rate).
  • Frequently pulls animals from open-admission shelters to relieve overcrowding — the two models are usually collaborators, not rivals.
  • "No-kill" is a live-release-rate statistic, not a promise that zero animals are ever euthanized — behaviorally dangerous or untreatable/suffering animals may still be humanely euthanized under most no-kill definitions.
// The license that actually matters in Colorado

What a PACFA license tells you

Colorado doesn't license organizations as "no-kill" or "open-admission" — that's industry language, not a legal category. What the state does regulate is the Pet Animal Care and Facilities Act (PACFA), run by the Colorado Department of Agriculture. Any shelter, rescue, sanctuary, or foster-based rescue operating in Colorado is required to hold a current PACFA license and pass annual inspection.

Animal Shelter
Houses animals at a central facility — small (<3,000 transfers/yr) or large (>3,000/yr)
Animal Rescue
Places animals in adoptive homes, no central facility — small (<99 transfers/yr) or large (>99/yr)
Animal Sanctuary
Private facility that houses animals long-term, not for impoundment purposes

Anyone housing more than 15 dogs and/or cats without a license is operating illegally under PACFA — CDA has pulled dozens of animals from unlicensed "rescues" and sanctuaries in cease-and-desist actions. A current PACFA license is the single easiest thing you can verify before you donate.

How to fill in the real numbers, fast

1) Cross-check every org against the state's live PACFA facility list — it's a public spreadsheet, updated by the state, license type included. 2) Pull intake / live-release numbers from each org's own published annual report, or from Shelter Animals Count, the national database shelters voluntarily report to. 3) For euthanasia policy (including fear-free / humane end-of-life practices), that's not public data anywhere — email or call each org and ask directly, then cite the org as the source on the site. I'm not going to let this page assert a policy for a real organization that I haven't heard from them directly.

// Why this exists

For homeless & at-risk pet owners, the pet is the reason they keep going

Some Colorado shelters already run safety-net programs — temporary boarding during domestic violence, hospitalization, treatment, or incarceration, so no one has to choose between safety and their animal. UNDERDOG CO's relief fund is built to sit next to those programs, not duplicate them.

01 — Goodie Bags

Food, a sweater or coat, a leash, basic flea/tick prevention, waste bags — built from donated inventory and handed out directly on Colorado streets.

02 — Vet Vouchers

The long-term goal: vouchers redeemable for vaccines, basic wellness exams, and spay/neuter at partner clinics — the #1 lever against surrender.

03 — Crisis Boarding Referrals

A clear, always-current list of Colorado orgs offering temporary safety-net boarding for people fleeing DV, entering treatment, or hospitalized.

04 — Education & Resources

Plain-language info on low-cost vet clinics, pet-friendly shelters, and how to keep a pet through a housing crisis instead of surrendering.

Program status: early build. Voucher program is a stated goal, not yet live — this section will be updated as partner clinics and funding are confirmed.

// Who's behind this

About the founder

[ FOUNDER PHOTO — swap in ]
Dominique Na · Founder & CEO, UNDERDOG CO

Dominique Na is 27 and Colorado-based. She started out headed toward law school — and left pre-law when she realized the work was draining the life out of her. She found her real calling in animal welfare, where the hours are longer and the pay is worse, but the work actually means something to her.

Before any of that, Dominique left a domestic abuse relationship and lived out of her car with her chihuahua by her side. The fear of losing him if she couldn't provide for him was one of the most frightening periods of her life — and it became the thing that pushed her to rebuild. That experience is the entire reason UNDERDOG CO exists: because she knows firsthand that for someone on the edge of homelessness, the fear of losing your pet can be as consuming as the fear of losing everything else.

Through adoptions, TNR work, and her time in veterinary and animal welfare organizations, Dominique estimates she's had a direct hand in saving roughly 900 animals. UNDERDOG CO is the next chapter of that work — built for the people and animals other programs weren't built for.
